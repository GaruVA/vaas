# Chapter 1 — Introduction

## 1.1 Background

The reliable verification of vehicular attendance at large industrial facilities represents a persistent operational challenge that sits at the intersection of physical security, human resources administration, and financial integrity. In heavy industrial settings — shipyards, port facilities, and manufacturing complexes — employees and subcontractor personnel frequently arrive as inseparable human–vehicle units: the vehicle is the attendance record, the billing identifier, and the primary accountability token for fuel allowance schemes. Systems designed for pedestrian workforce management are architecturally incapable of addressing this context, creating a well-documented gap between declared and auditable attendance (Pawar et al., 2021).

Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) has been the dominant technology for vehicle-based access control in industrial settings for over two decades. However, RFID deployments in electromagnetically dense environments — where multiple independent systems operate on adjacent or overlapping UHF frequency bands — exhibit systematic reliability failures. Rezaie et al. (2023) documented that simultaneous reader operation in dense industrial environments creates reader-to-tag collisions and reader-to-reader interference that cannot be fully mitigated through standard TDMA or FDMA multiplexing schemes. Knapp and Uckelmann (2022) reviewed RFID interference across complex logistics and production environments, concluding that metallic infrastructure, high-power industrial machinery, and adjacent RF systems collectively degrade UHF read reliability to the point where the recorded data cannot be considered an accurate proxy for physical presence. At the architectural level, RFID access control systems authenticate the credential token, not the physical vehicle; this distinction enables a class of behavioural exploitation — credential sharing, proxy scanning, and pedestrian badge-swipes — that is indistinguishable from legitimate system noise in environments where false positives are endemic.

Automatic Licence Plate Recognition (ALPR) resolves the architectural flaw directly: the licence plate is an optically persistent vehicle-bound identifier that cannot be separated from the vehicle, is immune to electromagnetic interference, and cannot be shared or proxied. Third-generation ALPR systems, exemplified by YOLOv8-based single-stage detection (Jocher et al., 2023), achieve real-time inference on edge hardware with plate detection accuracies exceeding 94 percent (Sabir et al., 2023) and end-to-end recognition accuracies above 93 percent under adverse lighting and weather conditions (Al-Dabbagh et al., 2024). These advances make ALPR a technically viable replacement for compromised RFID infrastructure in industrial facilities.

## 1.2 The Problem at Colombo Dockyard PLC

Colombo Dockyard PLC (CDL) is Sri Lanka's largest ship repair and shipbuilding enterprise, operating continuously across three eight-hour shifts within the Port of Colombo. Founded in 1974, CDL entered a joint venture with Onomichi Dockyard Co. Ltd. of Japan in 1992, which holds a majority equity stake of approximately 51 percent (CDL Annual Report, 2024). The facility operates four graving drydocks accommodating vessels up to 125,000 DWT, processes over 200 vessels annually, and employs approximately 1,400 to 3,000 permanent employees and trainees, supplemented by subcontractor personnel during major vessel refit cycles. CDL administers a contractual fuel allowance programme under which employees who bring personal vehicles to work are entitled to a monthly payment contingent on verifiable vehicle attendance.

CDL's existing vehicle attendance infrastructure relied on a UHF RFID system. The system failed on two concurrent fronts. The Colombo Harbour Authority (Sri Lanka Ports Authority, SLPA) operates an independent RFID access control system within the adjacent port on identical UHF frequency bands, generating systematic ID collision — harbour authority access cards are inadvertently detected at CDL's gate, creating false-positive attendance logs — and dual-card ambiguity, where employees carrying credentials for both facilities generate conflicting signals that prevent reliable identification (CDL Internal Audit, Finance Department, 2023). The resulting noise created an environment in which a second, behavioural failure mode became entrenched: because the RFID system authenticated the card rather than the vehicle, employees identified that scanning their RFID credential while entering as pedestrians, passengers in other vehicles, or on public transport produced attendance records indistinguishable from legitimate vehicle entries (CDL Internal Audit, Finance Department, 2023).

The compounded impact was quantifiable. An internal audit by CDL's Finance Department identified a consistent 15 to 20 percent discrepancy between RFID-recorded attendance and independently conducted vehicle parking-lot occupancy counts. This discrepancy translated to an estimated 225,000 to 300,000 LKR per month in unjustified fuel allowance payments — approximately 2.7 to 3.6 million LKR annually (CDL Internal Audit, Finance Department, 2023). The operational burden was equally significant: the Finance Department allocated approximately 20 hours per month to investigating disputed allowance claims. During peak shift-change windows — in which over 200 vehicles arrive within a 30-minute period — manual verification at the gate requires 30 to 45 seconds per vehicle, rendering systematic checking operationally infeasible and compelling security personnel to abandon the process entirely to prevent traffic gridlock.

## 1.3 Aim and Objectives

The aim of this project was to design, implement, and evaluate a computer-vision-based vehicle attendance and analytics system — the Vehicle Attendance and Analytics System (VAAS) — deployed specifically for Colombo Dockyard PLC, providing accurate, shift-aware, project-attributed, and cryptographically auditable records of vehicle presence at CDL's facility gates.

The following objectives were established:

- **O1 — High-Accuracy Plate Recognition:** Develop a Sri Lanka-specific ALPR pipeline using YOLOv8 plate detection, CLAHE contrast enhancement (Suleman et al., 2022), and a custom 37-class character classifier, achieving end-to-end recognition accuracy of at least 90 percent on CDL gate conditions.
- **O2 — Shift-Aware Attendance Engine:** Implement an attendance engine that records ENTRY and EXIT events against CDL's three-shift schedule (07:00–15:00, 15:00–23:00, 23:00–07:00), computes dwell time, classifies attendance status, and applies the 15-minute grace period reflecting Port of Colombo entry-queue conditions.
- **O3 — CDL Operational Context:** Extend the attendance system with CDL-specific features: drydock zone topology, vessel-project attribution, subcontractor company management, and per-project attendance summaries to support the fuel allowance calculation and subcontractor billing verification.
- **O4 — Tamper-Evident Audit Trail:** Implement a SHA-256 hash-chained audit log in which each access record is cryptographically bound to its predecessor, ensuring that attendance data submitted to HR and Finance is mathematically verifiable and dispute-proof.
- **O5 — Reporting and Web Interface:** Deliver a role-based web dashboard (Gate Operator, Manager, Admin) with real-time gate alerts, and generate the personal-vehicle allowance report, OHS compliance report, and gate rejection audit required by CDL operational stakeholders.
